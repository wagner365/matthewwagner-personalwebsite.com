Houses of Oxford

My work
Brand identity, logo, and 52-week revenue/inventory model

Houses of Oxford is a hoodie brand built for Miami University's student body. I joined the refounding team to shape the brand's visual identity and to build the financial model the founders use to actually run the business — pricing, sales-rep commission, seasonality, and inventory reorder timing.

The first four weeks, honestly

The model isn't a projection dressed up to look good — it's built on real sales data from launch. In the first four weeks, the business sold 24 hoodies across 11 active sales reps for $960 in revenue, at a 37.5% gross margin. After paying out commission, EBITDA came in at just $18.82. That's a real, thin number, and the model shows it plainly instead of smoothing it over.

Where it broke

The break-even analysis was the uncomfortable part of the model to build: at a $0.78 contribution margin per unit after commission, the business needed roughly 13 units sold per week just to cover fixed costs. Actual sales were averaging 6 units per week — meaningfully below break-even. Rather than hide that in a rosier scenario, I built the model to surface it, because the founders needed the real number to make pricing and commission-structure decisions, not a number that felt better.

Learning: a financial model's job isn't to make the venture look good — it's to tell the founders what's actually true, especially when that's the uncomfortable answer.

Seasonality and inventory

Because Houses of Oxford sells almost exclusively to Miami students, the 52-week model applies seasonal multipliers tied to the academic calendar — full-strength sales during active semesters, near-zero volume over summer break, and a steep drop over winter break. I paired that with an inventory runway model that flags reorder points based on a two-week supplier lead time, so the team can see exactly when current stock (53 units at time of writing) will run out under each growth scenario.

Three growth scenarios

The model runs conservative (flat, 0% weekly growth), base case (5% weekly growth in active season), and optimistic (12% weekly growth) scenarios across a full year, so the founders can see the range of outcomes rather than a single number they have to trust blindly.

Product-level unit economics

Rather than modeling revenue as one blended number, I broke out gross margin per product — the 989 Hat, Dad Hat, and Trucker Hat run margins between 32% and 42%, while the tee and hoodie line run lower due to higher per-unit production cost. That level of detail is what let me see that hats, not apparel, are the actual margin driver for this business — which shaped how I prioritize farmers market inventory.

Break-even and the wholesale question

At current fixed costs of roughly $775 per year, the model puts break-even at about 87 units sold annually, or just over 7 units a month — a number small enough to hit through farmers markets alone. Wholesale (a potential deal with a local golf course) is modeled starting Year 2 only, since that relationship wasn't confirmed at the time I built the model. I didn't want to count revenue that wasn't real yet.

Learning: the temptation with your own venture is to model the deal you're hoping for. I built this to only count what's confirmed, and treat everything else as upside.

Flow — Heated Glove Venture

Team venture built for ESP 201: Intro to Entrepreneurship & Business Models, culminating in a live investor-style pitch.

Team
Owen Fankhauser, Tanner Lowe, Lexie Margelis, Jane Spelman, Matthew Wagner
Course
ESP 201 · Spring 2025
Result
2nd place, Entrepreneurship Road Test Pitch Competition

Flow was a semester-long venture project built around a heated glove designed for people who work or play outside in the cold — construction workers, winter sports athletes, and outdoor laborers. The product's key differentiator was an internal heat-transfer pipe design meant to preserve hand mobility and fine motor skill, which most heated-glove competitors sacrifice for warmth.

The full build-out

Over the semester our team moved through prototype testing and customer validation, a full market opportunity analysis identifying four direct competitors, a business model analysis narrowing our target markets from a too-broad initial list down to winter sports, medical, and construction, and a final live pitch built around TAM/SAM/SOM sizing and a differentiation narrative.

The mistake we caught before it mattered

Heading into final pitch practice, we made a deliberate call to rework our financial section after realizing our first pass leaned on numbers that would have gotten us an easy grade but weren't actually realistic. We chose to redo the TAM/SAM/SOM breakdown with numbers we could defend under questioning, even though it meant more work close to the deadline. That decision — prioritizing being right over looking impressive — is the single biggest thing I took from the project.

Learning: an impressive-looking number that falls apart under one follow-up question is worse than a modest number you can actually defend. We rebuilt ours with three days left rather than present numbers we didn't believe.

Sourcing and prototyping problems

Early on, sourcing glove liners and heat-transfer materials that fit our budget was a real obstacle — several of our first tests failed outright, and we had to rebuild our prototype demonstration approach for the in-class presentation after realizing our original plan didn't translate well live. We adjusted by having one team member narrate what was happening inside the glove during the demo, since the mechanism itself wasn't visible.

Outcome

The venture placed 2nd in the 2025 Entrepreneurship Road Test Pitch Competition — a result built on a semester of test cards that didn't work, a financial model we scrapped and rebuilt, and a pitch we rewrote more than once to make it defensible under real questions.
